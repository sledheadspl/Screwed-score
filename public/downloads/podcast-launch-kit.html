Section 01

Podcast Strategy Foundation

Most podcasts fail before they release a second season — not because the host is boring or the audio is bad, but because they launched without a clear strategy. They chose a topic too broad, a format that doesn't suit their natural energy, and a name that tells no one anything. This section gives you a framework to make the foundational decisions correctly the first time.

Niche Selection Framework

The best podcast niche sits at the intersection of three things: what you know deeply, what a specific audience needs, and what existing podcasts are failing to deliver. Your niche is not your topic — it is your specific angle on a topic for a specific listener.

Niche Formula: [TOPIC] + [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE] + [UNIQUE ANGLE OR FORMAT] Weak: "Marketing podcast" Strong: "Marketing strategy for independent consultants who hate social media" Weak: "Personal finance" Strong: "Money conversations for women in their 30s navigating salary negotiation" Weak: "Entrepreneurship" Strong: "Weekly 20-minute case studies on businesses that failed and what they learned"

Niche Selection Exercise

1
List 5 topics you could talk about for 2 hours without notes
These are your genuine domains of knowledge or passion. Do not filter for "market demand" yet. Just brainstorm freely.
2
For each topic, identify the specific listener who needs it most
Not "entrepreneurs" — "first-time founders with technical backgrounds who struggle with marketing." The more specific your listener, the more that listener will feel the show was made for them personally.
3
Search Apple Podcasts and Spotify for your top 2 topics
Look at the top 20 shows in the niche. What format do they all use? What are they all missing? What angle is not being covered? Your differentiation lives in the gaps you find.
4
Validate with a one-sentence pitch test
Write: "This podcast is for [SPECIFIC PERSON] who wants [SPECIFIC OUTCOME] and is tired of [COMMON FRUSTRATION WITH EXISTING OPTIONS]." If you cannot fill that in convincingly, narrow the niche further.

Show Format Decision Tree

Format Best If You Are... Episode Length Production Level Guest Dependency
Solo deep-diveAn expert, educator, or strong storyteller15–45 minLowNone
One-on-one interviewA great listener and conversationalist30–60 minMediumHigh
Co-hosted / banterPart of a natural duo with a co-host30–60 minMediumLow
Panel discussionA community connector or industry insider45–75 minHighVery High
Narrative / documentaryA writer, journalist, or storyteller20–45 minVery HighMedium
News roundupAn industry follower with hot takes15–30 minLow–MediumNone
💡
Format caution: Interview formats are the most popular but the hardest to differentiate. If you choose interviews, your differentiation must come from guest selection quality, the depth of your research, or a genuinely unique question format — not just the topic. "I ask questions other podcasters don't" only works if you actually do.

Competitive Positioning

Map your 3–5 closest competitors on two axes: (1) Beginner vs. Advanced audience, (2) Practical vs. Inspirational content. Find the quadrant that is underserved. This is your positioning. Put it in your show description, your cover art aesthetic, and your episode titles.

Positioning Statement Template
"[SHOW NAME] is the podcast for [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE] who want [SPECIFIC OUTCOME]. Unlike [COMPETITOR TYPE], we [UNIQUE DIFFERENTIATOR]. Every [EPISODE CADENCE], you'll get [FORMAT PROMISE]. No [COMMON FRUSTRATION WITH COMPETITORS]."

Example: "The Screwed Score Podcast is the show for independent creators who want real income data, not inspiration porn. Unlike most creator podcasts, we lead with numbers and skip the guru talk. Every Monday, you get one creator's real revenue breakdown. No fake transparency."

Naming Your Show

A strong podcast name is specific enough to communicate the niche, memorable enough to be shared by word of mouth, and searchable enough to appear when someone looks for it on a platform. A weak name is either generic (The Business Podcast) or overly clever in a way that tells listeners nothing (The Prism Show).

🔍
Name checklist before committing: (1) Is it under 4 words? (2) Does it tell someone what the show is about? (3) Can it be easily spelled from hearing it? (4) Is it available on Apple Podcasts and Spotify? (5) Can you get the .com domain or a close variant? (6) Is the social handle available on at least 2 platforms?
Section 02

Technical Setup Guide

You do not need expensive gear to launch a great podcast. You need the right gear for your budget and a recording environment that doesn't embarrass you. Listeners will tolerate a slightly imperfect microphone. They will not tolerate echo, background noise, or inconsistent volume. Environment matters more than equipment.

Budget Tier 1: $0–$200

🎤
$0–$200 Starter Setup
  • Microphone: Blue Yeti Nano ($99) or Samson Q2U ($60) — USB, plug-and-play. Samson Q2U is the better value: it has both USB and XLR output so it grows with you.
  • Headphones: Any closed-back headphones you own. Sony MDR-7506 ($90) if you need to buy. Avoid AirPods for monitoring — they colour the sound.
  • DAW (recording software): Audacity (free, Windows/Mac/Linux) or GarageBand (free, Mac). Both are fully capable for podcast production.
  • Remote recording: Riverside.fm free tier (separate audio tracks per participant) or Zencastr free tier. Record locally for best quality.
  • Editing: Audacity + free noise reduction plugins. Aim for under 2 hours editing per hour of raw audio before you consider outsourcing.

Budget Tier 2: $200–$800

🎤
$200–$800 Professional Setup
  • Microphone: Shure MV7 ($249) — USB/XLR hybrid, broadcast-quality, built-in headphone monitoring. The single best purchase at this budget tier.
  • Audio interface (if going XLR): Focusrite Scarlett Solo ($119) or Audient iD4 ($199). Required for XLR mics but adds professional preamp quality.
  • Headphones: Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro ($149) or Audio-Technica ATH-M50x ($149). Flat response, closed back, studio standard.
  • DAW: Hindenburg Journalist ($95/yr) — purpose-built for podcasters. Automatic voice leveling, loudness metering, easy export. Or continue with Audacity/GarageBand.
  • Remote recording: Riverside.fm Creator plan ($19/mo) — 4K video, separate audio tracks, local recording, transcript. Worth it for interview-based shows.
  • Pop filter + boom arm: RODE PSA1 arm ($99) + basic pop filter ($15). Keeps mic at consistent distance, removes plosives, looks professional on video.

Budget Tier 3: $800+

🎤
$800+ Studio-Grade Setup
  • Microphone: Shure SM7B ($399) — the industry standard for voice recording. Requires a good preamp due to low output. Add Cloudlifter ($149) if your interface doesn't have enough gain.
  • Audio interface: Universal Audio Volt 276 ($299) or Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 ($169). The UA Volt has a vintage transformer mode that adds warmth to voice recordings.
  • Acoustic treatment: 6–8 acoustic panels on walls and ceiling ($150–$300 DIY or $400 pre-made). Makes a larger sonic difference than mic upgrades above $300.
  • Headphones: Sennheiser HD 600 ($299) or Beyerdynamic DT 880 Pro ($199). Semi-open for mixing, closed for recording.
  • DAW: Adobe Audition ($55/mo with Creative Cloud) or Logic Pro ($199 one-time, Mac). Logic Pro is outstanding value for Mac users doing anything more than basic editing.
  • Video camera (optional): Sony ZV-E10 ($550) + Elgato Key Light ($199). For shows that repurpose to YouTube or for clip content on social media.

Recording Environment Checklist

The right room matters more than any gear under $500. Before your first recording session, work through this list:

  • Record in the smallest room available — smaller rooms have less reverb
  • Hang clothes in an open closet behind you — fabric absorbs echo without cost
  • Place a thick blanket or duvet on the floor under your recording position
  • Turn off HVAC 5 minutes before recording (you can turn it back on during breaks)
  • Close windows and doors. Check for external noise at the time of day you plan to record
  • Place the microphone 4–8 inches from your mouth, slightly off-axis to reduce plosives
  • Record a 30-second room tone (silence) at the start of each session for noise reduction
  • Use headphones while recording to catch any background noise in real time
  • Test record before every episode. Play it back on earbuds and speakers to check quality

Upload & Distribution Workflow

1
Export from DAW as 44.1kHz, 16-bit, mono WAV
Mono is correct for most voice-only podcasts. WAV preserves full quality before your host compresses to MP3.
2
Normalize loudness to -16 LUFS integrated
Apple Podcasts and Spotify normalize to -16 LUFS. If your file is louder, it gets turned down. Match the target and your episodes sound consistent across all platforms.
3
Upload to podcast host as MP3, 128kbps mono
Recommended hosts: Buzzsprout, Transistor, or Captivate. All provide an RSS feed for distribution. Buzzsprout has the best onboarding for new podcasters. Transistor is better for multi-show or team setups.
4
Add episode metadata before publishing
Title, description (use show notes template from Section 08), season/episode number, chapter markers if supported, transcript. Missing metadata reduces discoverability.

Cover Art Design Brief

Your cover art is the thumbnail that appears in every podcast app in the world. It is the first impression a potential listener gets before they ever hear your voice. Most podcast cover art fails for two reasons: it tries to communicate too much, or it looks like every other show in the category. This brief gives you the exact specs and principles to create art that stands out in a feed of thumbnails.

Universal rule: Design at 3000 x 3000px and export at 300 DPI. Your art will be displayed at sizes as small as 55 x 55px in some contexts (Apple Watch, sidebar in podcast apps). It must remain legible and identifiable at thumbnail scale. Test your art at 100px wide before finalizing.

Font Pairing Guide

Use a maximum of two fonts in your cover art. One for the show name (display/heading font) and one for the tagline or episode descriptor (body font). Test that both are legible at 100px wide.

Authority / Expertise Shows
Display: Bebas Neue, Oswald, or Montserrat Bold
Body: Inter or Source Sans Pro
Pair with: Dark background, high contrast
Avoid: Script fonts, anything with thin strokes
Conversational / Lifestyle Shows
Display: Playfair Display, Lora, or Abril Fatface
Body: Raleway or Nunito
Pair with: Warm, mid-saturation backgrounds
Avoid: All-caps in body text, overly condensed fonts
Tech / Startup / Analytical
Display: Space Grotesk, DM Sans Extra Bold, or Syne
Body: IBM Plex Sans or Inter
Pair with: Dark or neutral background, minimal color
Avoid: Decorative serifs, any font that reads as playful
Creative / Arts / Culture
Display: Anything with character — Righteous, Rubik, or custom display
Body: Libre Baskerville or Crimson Text
Pair with: Unexpected color combinations, texture
Avoid: Playing it safe — this category rewards bold choices

Section 04

The 8 Episode Structure Templates

Every episode structure below is a complete run-of-show. Times are guidelines — adjust to your target episode length. Use these as starting scripts for your first few episodes, then adapt them once you have found your natural rhythm. Consistent structure builds listener habit: when people know what to expect, they come back.

Template 1: Solo Deep-Dive

Best for: expertise-based shows, educational content, opinion pieces. Host speaks directly to audience with no guests. Requires the most preparation but is also the most independent to produce.

Solo Deep-Dive Run-of-Show (Target: 25 min)
0:00–0:45
Hook. Open with the most compelling sentence from the entire episode. A stat, a counterintuitive claim, a story teaser. Do NOT start with "Welcome to [show name]."
0:45–2:00
Promise + brief intro. "Today I'm going to show you exactly [SPECIFIC OUTCOME]." Then 30 seconds of show/host intro for new listeners.
2:00–4:00
Context / Setup. Why this topic matters right now. The problem, the question, the gap you are filling. Set the stakes.
4:00–20:00
Main content (3-4 segments). Divide your core content into 3–4 named sections. Announce each transition: "The second thing I want to cover is..." Treat each section as a mini-episode within the episode. Include one specific, memorable example per section.
20:00–22:30
Summary + Key Takeaways. Briefly restate the 3 most important points. "If you take nothing else from today..."
22:30–24:00
CTA. ONE call to action. Subscribe, leave a review, visit a URL, join the email list. Never more than one. Close with what's coming next episode.
24:00–25:00
Outro. Brief sign-off. Consistent every episode. 30–45 seconds maximum. Sponsor spot can go here or at top of episode.

Template 2: One-on-One Interview

Best for: networking-based shows, learning from experts, story-driven content. Requires strong pre-interview research and a clear guest brief sent in advance. The quality of your questions determines the quality of the episode.

Interview Episode Run-of-Show (Target: 45 min)
0:00–1:00
Teaser clip. 30–60 seconds of the most compelling moment from the interview. Pull it in editing. Best line, most surprising answer, strongest moment. Opens the episode mid-conversation.
1:00–3:00
Host intro + guest intro. "I'm [HOST], this is [SHOW]. Today's guest is [GUEST NAME] — [2-3 sentence bio that explains why they're worth listening to]. What we cover today: [3 bullet points]."
3:00–7:00
Origin story. "Before we get into [main topic], walk me through how you got to where you are today. The short version." Let the guest talk. Interrupt only to clarify, not to redirect.
7:00–35:00
Core questions (4–6 prepared). Pre-write 8 questions, use 4–6. Leave room for follow-up. The best interviews sound like conversations, not questionnaires. After each answer, ask one genuine follow-up before moving to next prepared question.
35:00–40:00
Rapid-fire / lightning round (optional). 5 quick questions, 1-sentence answers. Creates memorable, shareable moments. "Biggest mistake. Best book. Advice to your past self."
40:00–43:00
Guest CTA. "Where can listeners find you / follow your work / get the resource you mentioned?" Give the guest 60 seconds to direct your audience.
43:00–45:00
Host outro + main CTA. Thank guest, your one listener CTA, next episode preview, sign-off.

Template 3: Panel Discussion

Best for: roundtable debates, industry discussions, multi-perspective topics. Hardest to produce well — requires a strong facilitator to prevent crosstalk and ensure equal airtime.

Panel Discussion Run-of-Show (Target: 55 min)
0:00–3:00
Host intro + panelist intros. One sentence per panelist. Do not read full bios — have them in the show notes. Frame the debate topic clearly: "Today we're debating [TOPIC]. [GUEST A] argues [POSITION A]. [GUEST B] argues [POSITION B]."
3:00–10:00
Opening statements. 90–120 seconds each panelist states their core position. No interruptions. Host moderates order.
10:00–40:00
Facilitated discussion (3 main questions). Pre-write 5 questions, plan to cover 3. Each question gets one opening answer (1 min) then open discussion (5 min). Host's job: redirect when panelists talk past each other, invite quieter panelists explicitly, maintain time discipline.
40:00–50:00
Audience questions or pre-submitted questions. 3–5 questions. Tell guests in advance you'll include these so they can prepare thoughtful answers.
50:00–53:00
Closing statements. 60 seconds each panelist to summarize their key position. Has any opinion changed? What's their final recommendation?
53:00–55:00
Host summary + outro. Host summarizes the 3 key takeaways from the discussion. CTA + sign-off.

Template 4: Listener Q&A

Best for: community-driven shows, trust-building, audience engagement. Collect questions via email, social media, or a Typeform link. Announce the Q&A episode 1 week in advance to collect enough material.

Q&A Episode Run-of-Show (Target: 30 min)
0:00–1:30
Intro. "This is our Q&A episode for [MONTH]. I collected [X] questions and I'm answering the [10] best ones today. Let's get into it."
1:30–27:00
Questions (10 questions, ~2.5 min each). Read the question, credit the asker by first name, give your answer. Group thematically if questions cluster around a topic. Your most detailed answer should be your middle one, not your last.
27:00–29:00
Theme wrap + CTA. "A theme I noticed in today's questions is [THEME]. That tells me [AUDIENCE INSIGHT]." Then CTA: "Submit your question for next Q&A at [URL / email / form]."
29:00–30:00
Outro.

Template 5: Case Study

Best for: results-driven shows, business/career podcasts, investigative content. Walk through one specific example in depth. Listeners learn principles through concrete stories, not abstractions.

Case Study Run-of-Show (Target: 30 min)
0:00–1:00
Hook with outcome. State the result first: "By the end of this episode, you'll know exactly how [PERSON/COMPANY] went from [START STATE] to [END STATE]." Humans stay engaged when they know where the story is going.
1:00–5:00
Background. Who is the subject? What was the situation before? What problem were they trying to solve? Set the scene with specifics — numbers, dates, context.
5:00–20:00
The journey (3 phases). What did they try first? What failed and why? What pivot happened? What worked and why? Keep each phase to 5 minutes. Include specific data, quotes, and decision points.
20:00–26:00
Results + analysis. What happened? What were the measurable outcomes? What does this teach us that we couldn't have inferred without this specific case?
26:00–29:00
Listener application. "Here's how you can apply this in your own [situation]..." Give 2-3 actionable takeaways that go beyond the surface.
29:00–30:00
CTA + outro.

Template 6: News Roundup

Best for: fast-moving industries, tech, finance, creator economy. Short and consistent. Listeners come for your take, not just the news — ensure every story includes your actual opinion.

News Roundup Run-of-Show (Target: 20 min)
0:00–1:00
Intro. "Welcome to [SHOW]. This is the [DATE] edition. I've got [X] stories and [TIME] to cover them. Let's go." Efficiency signals respect for listener time.
1:00–17:00
5 stories (2.5–3 min each). Per story: (1) What happened — 30 seconds. (2) Why it matters — 60 seconds. (3) Your take / prediction — 60 seconds. Total = ~2.5 min. The take is what differentiates you from a news ticker.
17:00–19:00
Story of the Week. The one story you'd tell a friend. Slightly more opinion, slightly longer. This is the shareable moment of the episode.
19:00–20:00
Outro + next edition preview.

Template 7: Narrative Storytelling

Best for: documentary-style podcasts, long-form journalism, true crime. The most cinematic format. Requires the most production but creates the most loyal audiences.

Narrative Episode Run-of-Show (Target: 35 min)
0:00–1:30
Cold open. Drop into a scene from the middle of the story. No context yet. Create tension or mystery. End on a cliffhanger. Then: "I'm [HOST]. This is [SHOW]."
1:30–6:00
Setup / backstory. Now give context. Who is involved? Where and when does this take place? What are the stakes?
6:00–26:00
The narrative arc. Rising action → complication → crisis → resolution. Use scene transitions: "Six months earlier..." or "Meanwhile, across town...". Include interview clips if available. Write for the ear, not the eye: concrete sensory detail, short sentences, active verbs.
26:00–32:00
Resolution + meaning. What happened? What does this story mean? Why should the listener care? This is where the journalism meets the human truth.
32:00–35:00
Epilogue + CTA. Where are the people now? Brief update. Then your CTA and outro music.

Template 8: Repurposed Content

Best for: creators who have existing video, blog, or course content. Convert your best-performing content into podcast episodes to extend reach with minimal new creation.

Repurposed Content Run-of-Show (Target: 20 min)
0:00–1:00
Intro. "Today I'm sharing [CONTENT TYPE] that originally appeared on [PLATFORM]. I've updated it for this format and added [NEW ELEMENT]." Transparency builds trust and acknowledges cross-platform listeners.
1:00–16:00
Adapted content. Read and adapt your written content for the ear — rewrite all visual references ("as you can see" → "imagine"). Add voice colour: emphasis, pacing changes, brief ad-libs that were not in the original. This prevents the "reading a blog post" energy that kills engagement.
16:00–19:00
Expanded section. Add 2–3 minutes of material not in the original: new examples, updates since publication, listener questions about the topic.
19:00–20:00
CTA. Link to original content in show notes for listeners who want the written or video version.
Section 05

Pre-Launch Checklist (50 Points)

Do not publish your first episode until every item in this checklist is complete. Launching before you are ready means your first listeners get a broken or incomplete experience and never come back. You only get one first impression per listener.

Brand & Identity (9 items)
  • Show name finalized and verified unique on Apple Podcasts and Spotify
  • Show description written (150–300 words, keyword-rich, audience-first)
  • Cover art completed at 3000x3000px, tested at 100px, exported under 512KB
  • Show category selected (pick primary and secondary if available)
  • Language and country set correctly in your podcast host settings
  • Show website or landing page live (even a single page is fine)
  • Social media handles claimed on Instagram, Twitter/X, and one other platform
  • Show email address created (hello@[showname].com or a Gmail variant)
  • Intro/outro music produced or licensed (royalty-free: Epidemic Sound, Artlist)
Technical Setup (10 items)
  • Podcast hosting account created and configured (Buzzsprout, Transistor, or Captivate)
  • RSS feed generated and validated at castfeedvalidator.com
  • Custom domain connected to podcast host (optional but recommended)
  • Recording environment tested — no echo, no hum, no background noise
  • Audio levels calibrated — recorded levels peak between -12 and -6 dBFS
  • Loudness target set: -16 LUFS integrated for podcast export
  • MP3 export settings confirmed: 128kbps, 44.1kHz, mono (or stereo if music show)
  • Remote recording platform tested with a mock interview (Riverside, Zencastr, or Squadcast)
  • Backup recording method established (always record locally AND in the cloud)
  • Episode file naming convention established: [SHOWCODE]-EP[NUM]-[GUEST-LASTNAME].mp3
Content (12 items)
  • Trailer episode recorded (60–90 seconds: what the show is, who it's for, why they should subscribe)
  • Minimum 3 episodes recorded, edited, and ready to publish on launch day
  • Episode 1 is your strongest content — it sets the bar for everything that follows
  • Show notes template created and applied to all launch episodes (see Section 08)
  • Timestamps written for each launch episode
  • Episode titles written: descriptive, specific, include a searchable keyword
  • Intro script recorded and mixed into each episode
  • Outro script recorded and mixed into each episode
  • All audio files QC'd: listen to the full episode on earbuds before uploading
  • Episode artwork created if using individual episode art (not required but improves click-through)
  • Transcripts generated for at least your first 3 episodes (Otter.ai, Descript, or Riverside auto-transcribe)
  • Content calendar built for first 8 episodes with topic and guest (if applicable) confirmed
Platform Submissions (8 items)
  • Apple Podcasts submitted via Apple Podcasters Connect — approval takes 24–72 hours
  • Spotify submitted via Spotify for Podcasters — approval is usually same-day
  • Google Podcasts / YouTube Music submitted (note: Google Podcasts merged into YouTube Music in 2024 — submit your RSS there)
  • Amazon Music & Audible submitted at podcasters.amazon.com — approval 3–5 business days
  • iHeart Radio submitted at iheartmedia.com/podcasts — approval 1–2 weeks
  • Pocket Casts submitted via pocketcasts.com/submit
  • Overcast submission (auto-discovers from Apple Podcasts once approved)
  • Podcast Index submitted at podcastindex.org for open ecosystem coverage
Analytics & Monetization Prep (6 items)
  • Podcast host analytics enabled and understood: downloads, listener retention, top episodes
  • Apple Podcasters analytics account connected to see Apple-specific listener data
  • Spotify for Podcasters analytics reviewed — separate and valuable data set
  • Email list integration set up: new subscriber form on your website linked to podcast content
  • Anchor/Patreon/Buy Me A Coffee page created if planning listener support from day one
  • Sponsor wish list of 5 target brands written down (you will need this for Section 07)
Launch Readiness (5 items)
  • Launch date set and non-negotiable — perfection is the enemy of launch
  • Launch Week Playbook (Section 06) reviewed and social posts scheduled
  • Email to existing list drafted and scheduled for launch day
  • Guest outreach emails drafted for first 5 interview guests (if applicable)
  • Review ask script prepared for launch week (see Section 06)
Section 06

Launch Week Playbook (Days 1–7)

The first 7 days of your podcast determine its long-term visibility. Apple Podcasts and Spotify both surface new shows to potential listeners in their first 30 days. The more reviews, listens, and followers you generate in launch week, the better placement you get in "New and Noteworthy" sections. Here is a day-by-day action plan with exact copy.

Day 1 — Launch Day
Go live & announce everywhere simultaneously
  • Publish episodes 1, 2, and 3 on your podcast host simultaneously (staggered release confuses the algorithm — publish all three at once for launch)
  • Email your existing list (see email template below)
  • Post on every social channel: Instagram, Twitter/X, LinkedIn, TikTok, Facebook Group if applicable
  • Text 10 friends personally and ask them to subscribe and leave a review today specifically

    Section 08

    Show Notes Template System

    Show notes serve two purposes: they give listeners a reference document, and they give search engines a reason to index your episode. Most podcasters write either no show notes (missed SEO opportunity) or lazy two-line summaries (wasted effort). This template gives you the structure to write show notes that rank in search and serve your listeners.

    SEO-Optimized Show Notes Structure

    Show Notes Template: [EPISODE NUMBER]: [EPISODE TITLE — 60 characters max, include primary keyword] [EPISODE HOOK — 2-3 sentences. The most compelling thing about this episode. Write this as if describing the episode to a friend who hasn't heard it.] In this episode: - [KEY POINT 1 — specific, not vague] - [KEY POINT 2] - [KEY POINT 3] - [KEY POINT 4] - [KEY POINT 5] [EXPANDED DESCRIPTION — 150-300 words. This is where SEO lives. Use your topic keywords naturally. Summarize the main arguments or insights. Do not just say "we discuss X." Say what the actual insight or conclusion was. Treat this like a blog post intro.] EPISODE TIMESTAMPS: 00:00 — Introduction [MM:SS] — [TOPIC / SEGMENT NAME] [MM:SS] — [TOPIC / SEGMENT NAME] [MM:SS] — [GUEST NAME intro, if applicable] [MM:SS] — [KEY TOPIC 1] [MM:SS] — [KEY TOPIC 2] [MM:SS] — [KEY TOPIC 3] [MM:SS] — Rapid fire / closing questions [MM:SS] — Where to find [GUEST NAME] [MM:SS] — Outro RESOURCES MENTIONED: - [Tool / Book / Article name] — [brief description] — [URL] - [Tool / Book / Article name] — [brief description] — [URL] - [Guest's website/book/course] — [URL] CONNECT WITH [GUEST NAME]: - Website: [URL] - Twitter/X: [@handle] - LinkedIn: [URL] - [Other platform]: [URL] CONNECT WITH [SHOW NAME]: - Website: [SHOW URL] - Newsletter: [SIGNUP URL] - Instagram: [@handle] - Twitter/X: [@handle] SUBSCRIBE & REVIEW: Apple Podcasts: [DIRECT LINK] Spotify: [DIRECT LINK] → If you enjoyed this episode, leaving a review on Apple Podcasts takes 2 minutes and helps new listeners find the show. [CALL TO ACTION — one specific action you want listeners to take. E.g., "Download the free [LEAD MAGNET] at [URL]" or "Join 3,000+ subscribers at [NEWSLETTER URL]"]

    Timestamp Format Guide

    Use MM:SS format for episodes under 1 hour (07:34), and H:MM:SS for episodes over 60 minutes (1:07:34). Apple Podcasts, Spotify, and most modern podcast apps will make timestamps clickable if formatted correctly in the description field. Test your timestamps are clickable before publishing.

    ⚠
    Timestamp writing tip: Write timestamps from the listener's perspective, not the production perspective. "05:22 — The email marketing section" tells a listener nothing. "05:22 — Why 90% of email sequences fail in the first week" makes them want to jump to that moment. Strong timestamps increase episode replays and improve completion rates.

    Resource Link Section Best Practices

    Every resource mentioned in the episode should appear in show notes with a brief descriptor and a working URL. Do not just list tool names — include what the tool does and why it was mentioned. This is your affiliate link placement opportunity (with proper disclosure) and it is also the section listeners return to most frequently after an episode.

    Call-to-Action Placement

    Place your primary CTA in two positions: immediately after the episode hook (within the first visible paragraph, before the "read more" fold in podcast apps), and at the very end of the show notes after the social links. The top placement captures listeners who want to act immediately. The bottom placement captures those who read through to the end.

    🌟
    Show notes SEO tip: The episode title and first 150 characters of your description are what appear in search results on Apple Podcasts and Google. Write both to be compelling and keyword-relevant. "Episode 47" is a wasted title slot. "Episode 47: How to Negotiate Your First Podcast Sponsorship ($0 to $2K/month)" is an SEO asset.
    Section 09

    Growth & Monetization Roadmap (Months 1–6)

    Most podcasters grow by accident — or don't grow at all. This roadmap treats your podcast like a product with deliberate growth levers. Each month has a primary focus so you're not trying to do everything at once. Follow the sequence and you will have a monetized show with a genuine audience by the end of month six.

    Month 1
    Establish consistency & build the core habit
  • Publish on schedule, no exceptions. Consistency builds listener habit faster than quality does in month one.
  • Record 2–3 episodes ahead so you always have a buffer. Missing a week kills momentum and listener trust.
  • Focus on one social platform only. Trying to be everywhere in month one means being mediocre everywhere.
  • Send every episode to your email list with a short personal note about why you made this specific episode.
  • Target: 10 reviews on Apple Podcasts by end of month. Ask directly and repeatedly — it works.
    • Month 2
    Guest strategy & first cross-promotions
  • Book your first 4 interview guests if you are doing an interview format. Target people with audiences of 5,000–50,000 followers in adjacent niches — big enough to bring new listeners but small enough to say yes to you.
  • Set up a "guest swap" with 2–3 shows in adjacent niches of similar size. You appear on their show, they appear on yours. Most efficient listener growth tactic at this stage.
  • Repurpose every episode into 3 short clips for social. Use Descript, Riverside, or OpusClip for automatic clipping.
  • Start collecting listener questions for a Q&A episode in month 3. This builds community signal and episode content simultaneously.
    • Month 3
    Community building & first monetization test
  • Launch a free community for your listeners: a Discord server, a Slack group, or a simple newsletter reply thread. Even 20 engaged community members creates a feedback loop that drives better content.
  • Publish your first Q&A episode. Use listener names when referencing their questions. This creates a personal stake for those listeners and makes them brand advocates.
  • Reach out to your first 3 potential sponsors (use Section 07 templates). You do not need to be at 10,000 downloads. Start pitching at 500–1,000 downloads/episode to local businesses, micro-brands, or the tools you already recommend.
  • Evaluate your top 3 episodes by downloads and completion rate. What pattern do they share? Make more of that.
    • Month 4
    SEO content strategy & email list growth
  • Publish full show notes for every episode as a blog post on your website. 2,000-word posts with transcripts drive search traffic independently of podcast platform algorithms.
  • Create a podcast-specific lead magnet: a checklist, template, or resource guide that extends the most popular episode. Put this behind an email opt-in.
  • Set up a YouTube channel for your podcast. Upload the full audio with a static image or auto-generated audiogram. YouTube is a search engine; podcast episodes indexed there bring new listeners who would never find you on Spotify.
  • Close your first sponsorship deal, even if small. The learning from your first sponsor relationship — creative brief, tracking, renewal conversation — is worth more than the money.
    • Month 5
    Paid membership & premium content
  • Launch a paid tier for your most engaged listeners via Supercast, Patreon, or Memberful. Offer bonus episodes, ad-free listening, early access, or community access. Even 50 subscribers at $9/month = $450 MRR before any sponsor income.
  • Survey your audience about what kind of bonus content they'd pay for. Launch with what they asked for, not what you assumed they wanted.
  • Start pitching 2–3 brands per week for sponsorship. Treat it like a sales pipeline: track outreach, follow-ups, and responses in a simple spreadsheet.
  • Evaluate whether your current format and cadence is sustainable. Burn-out is the #1 reason podcasts stop. Adjust episode length or frequency now if needed — consistency at a lower cadence beats perfectionism at a higher one.
    • Month 6
    Systematize & scale
  • Build a production SOP so you can delegate or batch: recording day, editing day, publish day, social clip day. Schedule all four into your calendar as repeating blocks.
  • Hire a part-time editor if editing is taking more than 2 hours per episode. At $25–$50/episode for a freelance editor, this is the highest-ROI first hire for podcast growth.
  • Review 6-month analytics holistically: top episodes, listener retention by episode length, download sources, platform breakdown. These data points tell you where to invest in months 7–12.
  • Set month 12 goals now: downloads per episode, email list size, monthly sponsorship revenue, community size. Working backwards from targets is the only way to make them real.

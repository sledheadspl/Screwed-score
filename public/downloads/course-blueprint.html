Section 02

Curriculum Architecture Framework

Most creators build courses backwards — they list everything they know and turn it into lessons. The outcome-first method flips this. You start with the end result and reverse-engineer the path to get there.

The Outcome-First Design Method

1
Define the primary transformation
Write one sentence: "By the end of this course, students will be able to [specific action] so that [specific result]." Example: "By the end of this course, students will be able to launch a profitable Etsy shop generating $2,000/month so that they can leave their 9-5 within 12 months." If you can't write this sentence clearly, your course concept isn't ready.
2
Map the skill/knowledge prerequisites
Work backwards. What does the student need to know or have done immediately before achieving the final outcome? That's your last module. What do they need before that? Keep going until you reach where they are now — their starting point. Each step backward is a module.
3
Identify the confidence killers
What are the 3–5 things that would make a student quit before finishing? Identify them upfront and explicitly address each one in the curriculum. These become your "overcome the objection" lessons — positioned before the student encounters the obstacle.
4
Assign a win to every module
Every module must end with a student taking an action and having something to show for it — a completed template, a published post, a sent email, a configured tool. Courses fail when students feel like they're consuming without making progress. Build in visible wins.
5
Cut ruthlessly
Every lesson must answer yes to: "Does this directly move the student toward the primary transformation?" If it's interesting but not essential, cut it. You can release it as a bonus, a future module, or a separate product. Completion rates drop 40% for every extra hour of content above the optimal length.

Module Structure Formula

Every module in your course should follow this consistent structure so students know exactly what to expect:

MODULE STRUCTURE ───────────────────────────────────────── 1. Module Overview (2–3 min video) → What you'll learn in this module → Why it matters to the transformation → What you'll be able to do by the end 2. Core Lessons (3–7 lessons per module) → Each lesson = one concept or one action → Lesson structure: context → content → application → End every lesson with a single clear takeaway 3. Action Assignment (worksheet / template / task) → The specific thing they must do before module 3 → Tied directly to a real-world outcome 4. Module Recap (optional, 1–2 min) → Summary of key concepts → Preview of next module → Encouragement / re-commitment prompt

Lesson Types — When to Use Each

🎥
Video Lessons
  • Use for: demonstrations, walkthroughs, anything that benefits from seeing it done in real time
  • Ideal length: 8–15 minutes for instructional content; under 5 minutes for concept explanations
  • When to avoid: for reference material the student will return to repeatedly (use written guides instead)
  • Best practices: show your screen or your face — not both simultaneously unless you have high production value
📄
Worksheets & Templates
  • Use for: planning, strategy, brainstorming, anything requiring student input and reflection
  • Format: Google Doc or PDF with fillable fields — always include an example of a completed version
  • When to use: after a concept video, to help students apply the idea to their own situation
  • Power move: templates students can immediately use in their business (not just exercises)
✍
Quizzes & Knowledge Checks
  • Use for: reinforcing key concepts, identifying gaps before advancing to next module
  • Ideal placement: end of each module (not every lesson — that becomes annoying)
  • Format: 5–8 questions max; mix multiple choice with short answer
  • When to avoid: at the end of action-based modules where the output is the "test"
🏢
Live Calls & Q&A Sessions
  • Use for: cohort-based courses, premium tiers, troubleshooting, accountability
  • Frequency: weekly for 4–8 week cohorts; bi-weekly works for longer programs
  • Structure: 15-min recap → 30-min hot seat / Q&A → 15-min preview of next week
  • Record everything: replays are often more valuable than the live session for students in different time zones

Production Workflow

You do not need a professional studio to make a great course. You need consistent lighting, clear audio, and a workflow that keeps you moving. Here is the exact production system, from equipment to upload.

Equipment by Budget Tier

💰
Starter Tier — $0–$300 Total Budget
  • Camera: Your smartphone (iPhone 12+ or Android equivalent) on a $15 phone mount tripod
  • Microphone: BOYA BY-M1 lavalier mic ($25) — plugs directly into your phone's headphone jack or USB-C
  • Lighting: Sit facing a window. Natural daylight is free and looks better than cheap ring lights
  • Background: Decluttered wall, bookshelf, or use your screen recording only (no camera face)
  • Screen recording: OBS Studio (free) or Loom (free tier) for screen-based lessons
  • Editing: DaVinci Resolve (free) or iMovie (free on Mac) — learn 5 cuts and you're done
  • Slides: Google Slides (free) — use a dark background and 24pt+ font only
⚡
Mid-Tier — $300–$1,500 Total Budget
  • Camera: Sony ZV-E10 ($650) or Logitech Brio 4K webcam ($200) for desk-based recording
  • Microphone: Rode NT-USB Mini ($99) or Shure MV7 ($249) — USB direct to computer
  • Lighting: Elgato Key Light ($200) or equivalent — consistent, adjustable, no window dependency
  • Background: Dedicated corner of a room with intentional setup; acoustic panels optional ($80)
  • Screen recording: Screenflow ($129 Mac) or Camtasia ($300) — built-in editing, cleaner workflow
  • Editing: Screenflow or Final Cut Pro ($300 one-time) — significantly faster than DaVinci for tutorials
🆕
Pro Tier — $1,500–$5,000 Total Budget
  • Camera: Sony A7C ($1,800) or Sony ZV-E1 ($2,200) with a 35mm f/1.8 lens — cinematic depth of field
  • Microphone: Rode NT1 into Focusrite Scarlett Solo ($280 combo) — broadcast-quality audio
  • Lighting: Two-light setup: Elgato Key Light + Aputure MC fill light — professional separation
  • Acoustic treatment: Proper acoustic panels on recording wall — reduces reverb entirely
  • Teleprompter: Parrot Teleprompter ($150) — read scripts without looking away from lens
  • Editor: Hire a video editor on Fiverr or Upwork at $20–$50/hr after your content is proven

Recording SOP (Standard Operating Procedure)

1
Prepare your script or outline the night before
Never record without a script or detailed bullet outline. Scripts give you control. Bullets give you energy. Pick based on your delivery style. Write at a 6th-grade reading level — this is easier to speak and easier to understand.
2
Run a 30-second test recording first
Check audio levels (aim for peaks at –12dB to –6dB), confirm your lighting, verify your frame is right. This 30 seconds saves you from re-recording entire lessons due to a single technical issue you didn't catch.
3
Record in batches — 3–5 lessons per session
Your energy and delivery quality peak in the first 90 minutes of recording. Schedule 2-hour blocks. Do not try to record an entire module in one day — your voice gets tired and your performance drops noticeably by lesson 6.
4
Clap at the start of each take
This gives your editor a visual sync point for audio/video alignment and a clear marker of where each take begins. Say "Module 3, Lesson 2, Take 1" before each recording so your editor never has confusion about file identity.
5
Don't stop for small mistakes
Pause, take a breath, and repeat the sentence. Your editor will cut the mistake. Stopping mid-sentence and starting the whole lesson over wastes enormous time. The pause gives the editor a clean cut point.

Section 05

Pricing Strategy

Pricing is not about what your course is worth. It's about what transformation you're selling, who you're selling it to, and how you structure the offer. Most creators underprice their courses by 3–5x because they compare themselves to free YouTube content instead of the outcome they deliver.

The 4 Pricing Tiers

Tier 1 — Self-Study  $97 – $297

Core course content only: video lessons, worksheets, templates. No community, no live access, no coaching. The student gets the content and figures it out on their own.

When to use this tier: Your first launch, validating the content, or as the entry-level offer in a multi-tier stack. This tier builds your testimonials base.

What to include: All core modules + bonus module + resource library + lifetime access + updates for 12 months.

Positioning: "The complete system, at your own pace." Attract self-starters who are confident learners.

Tier 2 — With Community  $297 – $597

Everything in Tier 1 plus access to a private community (Circle, Slack, or Discord) where students ask questions, share progress, and hold each other accountable.

Why this works: Community increases completion rates from ~15% to 50%+. Students who complete courses leave testimonials. Testimonials sell more courses. The community pays for itself.

Your role: Post once per week in the community with a prompt, tip, or win spotlight. Respond to questions within 48 hours. This takes 30–60 minutes per week.

Positioning: "The system plus a room full of people doing it with you." Attract students who know they need accountability.

Tier 3 — With Coaching  $597 – $1,997

Everything in Tier 2 plus access to group coaching calls (bi-weekly or monthly). Students can submit questions in advance, get on hot seats, and receive direct feedback on their work.

Call structure: 60 minutes total — 15 min recap of the week's content, 35 min hot seat Q&A (3–4 students per call), 10 min wins and previews.

Limit group size: Cap this tier at 30–50 students per cohort to preserve call quality. Scarcity also helps conversion.

Positioning: "The system, the community, and direct access to [Your Name]." Attract serious students who want accountability from the expert, not just peers.

Tier 4 — VIP / Done-With-You  $2,000 – $10,000+

Everything in Tier 3 plus 1:1 access: private calls (2–4 per month), direct messaging access, personalized feedback on their work, and a custom roadmap for their specific situation.

Capacity: You can only support 3–8 VIP students at a time while maintaining your own business. Price accordingly.

Positioning: "We do this together. I treat your results like they're my own." Attract students for whom time is more valuable than money and who need a partner, not just a teacher.

Conversion path: VIP students often come from your Tier 2/3 alumni who completed the course and want to go deeper. Offer a "graduation upgrade" to your best students.

Price Anchoring Strategy

Always present your pricing in a tier stack on your sales page, even if you're only launching one tier. Showing a $2,000 option makes your $497 option feel like a deal. The anchor is the psychological baseline for evaluating value.

PRICE ANCHORING DISPLAY ORDER ───────────────────────────────────── [Most Expensive Tier] $2,000 "VIP" → shown first [Middle Tier] $497 "Complete" → shown second (YOUR TARGET) [Entry Tier] $197 "Essential" → shown third Result: The middle tier (your primary offer) appears as the rational, value-maximizing choice. Most buyers choose the middle. This is called the "Goldilocks effect" — not too cheap, not too expensive.

Beta Pricing — Your First Launch

🚀
Beta pricing strategy: For your first cohort, price at 40–60% of your intended full price in exchange for weekly feedback, testimonials, and the willingness to participate in a "live build." Beta students become your most loyal advocates because they feel ownership in the product.

Pre-Launch & Launch Timeline — 12 Weeks

This calendar takes you from idea validation through your first open cart. Every week has specific, non-negotiable actions. The entire system assumes you have zero audience and are building from scratch. If you already have an email list or social following, compress the first 4 weeks.

Weeks 1–2 — Validation Phase
Confirm demand before you build anything
  • Post the 5-question pre-sell survey to your social audience, email list, or relevant communities
  • Run 5–10 "discovery calls" — 20-minute conversations with ideal students asking about their problems
  • Score your course idea using the Idea Scoring Matrix from Section 1
  • Complete the competitive gap analysis — identify your positioning angle
  • Write your one-sentence course transformation statement
  • Decision gate: if fewer than 20 survey responses or score under 60, adjust concept before proceeding
Weeks 3–4 — Waitlist Build Phase
Announce and collect interested buyers before the course exists
  • Create a one-page "interest page" with your headline, outcome statement, and email opt-in form
  • Announce the course publicly: "I'm building [Course Name] — here's what it covers. Join the waitlist for early access and a 30% discount."
  • Post 3 pieces of "pre-launch content" per week: teach something from the course curriculum, prove your expertise
  • Send one email per week to your list about the problem your course solves — don't mention the course, just educate
  • Waitlist goal: 100+ email subscribers before proceeding to beta launch. If under 100, extend this phase by 2 weeks.
Weeks 5–7 — Content Production Phase
Build the course while continuing to build the audience
  • Record Welcome Module and Modules 1–3 first (enough for beta launch)
  • Build your worksheets and templates for Modules 1–3
  • Set up your course platform (Kajabi / Teachable / Podia) and upload content
  • Continue posting 3x per week on your primary social platform — show behind-the-scenes of building
  • Send 2 emails to your waitlist: one teaching a concept from the course, one "sneak peek" of the curriculum
  • Set up your sales page using the template in Section 6 — even if it's not perfect yet
Weeks 8–9 — Beta Cohort Phase
Sell to your waitlist at 50% off — your first paying students
  • Send the Beta Launch Email to your waitlist (see Section 8, Email 1): waitlist-only early access, 50% off, closes in 72 hours
  • Goal: 10–30 beta students paying. At $99 beta price, that's $990–$2,970 before you've finished building
  • Start your live beta cohort: release modules weekly, host weekly live Q&A calls
  • Finish recording Modules 4–6 during this phase — stay 1 week ahead of your cohort
  • Collect feedback after every module — use a 3-question form: What was most useful? What was confusing? What's missing?
  • Document every win, breakthrough, and "aha moment" from beta students — these become your testimonials
Weeks 10–11 — Pre-Launch Warm-Up
Build anticipation for the full launch with your broader audience
  • Post beta student results and testimonials publicly (with permission)
  • Send 3 pre-launch emails to your full list (Emails 2–4 in Section 8): problem story, student case study, cart-open announcement
  • Run a free live workshop or webinar on your primary content topic — pitch the course at the end
  • Open a new waitlist for non-beta buyers — collect 200+ new names if possible
  • Update your sales page with real testimonials from beta students
  • Finalize your price for full launch (remove beta discount — full price only)
Week 12 — Open Cart (5-Day Launch)
Sell hard for 5 days. The urgency is real and non-negotiable.
  • Day 1 (Monday): Cart Open email — "Doors are open" (Email 4). Post publicly: "Enrollment is open."
  • Day 2 (Tuesday): Social proof day — post 3 student testimonials across platforms
  • Day 3 (Wednesday): Mid-cart email — address the most common objection (Email 5). Live AMA on Instagram/YouTube.
  • Day 4 (Thursday): Urgency email — "2 days left" (Email 6). Focus on what they lose by not enrolling.
  • Day 5 (Friday): Final close — 2 emails: morning (12 hours left) and evening (2 hours left) (Email 7). Cart closes at midnight.
  • Post-launch: Send a "cart is closed" email to non-buyers with a waitlist for the next cohort

Section 09

Post-Launch Optimization

Most creators celebrate the launch, deliver the course, and then do nothing until the next launch. That's leaving 70% of the revenue on the table. Here's what to do in the 90 days after your first launch to set up the long game.

Testimonial Collection System

1
Send a "Week 2 Check-In" email to all students
Ask: "What's your biggest win from the first two modules?" This surfaces early results and gives you shareable content before the course is even done. Respond personally to every reply — this builds loyalty.
2
Send a formal testimonial request at end of course
Use a 4-question form: (1) What was your situation before the course? (2) What specific modules or lessons were most impactful? (3) What results have you achieved? (4) Who would you recommend this course to? This structure generates testimonials that sell.
3
Create a "Wins Board" in your community
Designate a channel or thread where students post results. Screenshot and share publicly (with permission). This creates a social proof engine that runs itself and makes other students more likely to share their own wins.
4
Reach out personally to top students for video testimonials
Identify your top 5–10 students. Send a personal voice note or email asking for a 60-second video testimonial. Provide a simple prompt: "Tell me who you are, what you were struggling with, what you did in the course, and what happened." Video testimonials convert 3–5x better than written ones.

Refund Handling Protocol

⚠
Expect a 2–8% refund rate on your first launch. This is normal. Do not take refunds personally — they are data. When processing a refund, always reply with: "Done — your refund is processed. I'd genuinely appreciate knowing what didn't work for you, if you're willing to share. No pressure." Most people will tell you. This feedback is gold for improving the next version.

Course Update Cadence

30 days post-launchQuick fixesRe-record any lessons that got consistent confusion questions. Add clarifying notes to worksheets.

Passive Sales Setup — Evergreen Webinar

Once you've run 2+ live launches and have 10+ strong testimonials, convert your launch process into an automated evergreen funnel. This is how course creators generate revenue without running launch campaigns.

⚡
Evergreen Webinar Funnel Architecture
  • Record a 45–60 minute "workshop" that teaches 3 high-value concepts from your course — end with a pitch for the full course
  • Host it on EverWebinar or WebinarKit ($99–$199/month) — simulates a live experience with automated "chat" and Q&A
  • Drive traffic via: a pinned post/link in bio, a lead magnet opt-in that redirects to the webinar registration, paid ads once you know your conversion rate
  • Set the webinar to run every 15–30 minutes so visitors can join "just starting" at any time
  • Create a 5-email post-webinar sequence for those who registered but didn't buy — use the email sequence from Section 8, compressed into 72 hours
  • A well-optimized evergreen webinar converts 3–8% of registrants into buyers — with a $497 course and 100 registrants/month, that's $1,500–$4,000/month on autopilot
📈
Revenue benchmark targets: Launch 1 (beta): $2,000–$8,000. Launch 2 (full launch): $10,000–$30,000. Launch 3+ (with social proof + warm list): $20,000–$80,000. Evergreen (monthly, no launch): $3,000–$15,000/month. These are achievable ranges for a solo creator with a sub-5,000 audience when execution is tight. The system is the multiplier — not the audience size.

Section 01

The 6-Phase Launch Framework

Most failed launches fail in the preparation phase — not the launch week itself. The cart opens and the creator wonders why no one is buying. The answer is almost always the same: not enough people knew, cared, or were primed. This framework eliminates that problem by treating launch as a 6-week campaign, not a 7-day event.

Phase Overview
Phase 1 — Pre-Pre-Launch (Weeks 1-4, 6 weeks before): Audience research, product validation, waitlist building, content strategy creation. The unsexy foundation that determines everything.

Phase 2 — Pre-Launch (Weeks 5-6): Intensive content push, email warm-up sequence (3 emails), social proof gathering, cart preview and urgency setup.

Phase 3 — Launch Week: Cart opens. 7-email sequence. Heavy content on all platforms. Daily monitoring and adjustment.

Phase 4 — Post-Launch (Weeks 1-2 after close): Buyer onboarding, social proof capture, refund policy execution, upsell opportunity.

Phase 5 — Optimization (Ongoing): Analyze what worked, fix what didn't, improve conversion rate for the next launch.

Phase 6 — Re-Launch: Evergreen or open/close cart model. Repeat the machine with improved assets and a larger list.
⚠
The 80% rule: 80% of your launch revenue will come from your email list, not your social media. Build your list before you build your product. A 1,000-person email list that knows you will consistently outperform a 50,000-follower social account with no list. Every week you delay building the list is a week of lost launch potential.
Section 02

Pre-Pre-Launch: Weeks 1–4

This is the phase nobody talks about and everybody skips. The creators who do this work consistently outperform those who don't — often by 3-5x on conversion rate.

Week 1: Audience Research and Validation

1
Run an audience survey
Send a 5-question survey to your email list or post in your community. Questions: (1) What's your #1 challenge with [topic]? (2) What have you already tried? (3) What would a "perfect solution" look like? (4) What would you pay for that solution? (5) What's your biggest fear about [topic]? Use Google Forms or Typeform. Aim for 30+ responses minimum before proceeding.
2
Mine comments, DMs, and forums
Spend 2 hours reading: your YouTube comments from the last 30 videos, your Instagram DMs, Reddit threads in your niche, relevant Facebook groups, Amazon reviews for books in your space. Copy exact phrases your audience uses to describe their problems. These phrases become your sales copy.
3
Validate the product concept
You need at least one of these three signals before building: (a) 50+ people have expressed this exact problem in their own words, (b) a competitor product on this topic is actively selling (market validation), or (c) someone has offered to pay you for this solution before you built it. If you have none of these signals, go back to research.
4
Write the sales page promise before building
Write the headline and first paragraph of your sales page before you build the product. This forces clarity on what you're actually selling. If you can't write a compelling headline, your product isn't defined enough yet. The headline format: "[Name of Product]: [What it is] that helps [target person] [achieve outcome] [in timeframe / without obstacle]."

Week 2: Waitlist Building Infrastructure

1
Build your waitlist landing page
One-page design. Elements: Headline (the transformation), 3 bullet points (what they'll get), who it's for (1 sentence), waitlist form (first name + email only), optional: early-bird incentive (10-20% discount for waitlist signups at launch). Tools: ConvertKit, MailerLite, or Carrd for the page.
2
Write the waitlist confirmation email
This email goes out immediately on signup. Content: Thank them, confirm what they signed up for, set expectations ("I'll email you [X] days before doors open with a special early-bird offer"), give them something now (a free tip, a piece of content, a resource) to reward the action and increase open rate for future emails.
3
Set up your email platform tags
Tag every waitlist signup with "Waitlist - [Product Name]" in your email platform. This segment gets your pre-launch sequence. Never send launch emails to your entire list without segmenting — irrelevant emails to uninterested subscribers tanks your deliverability.

Weeks 3–4: Content Strategy and List Growth

1
Publish 4 pieces of content directly related to your product topic
These pieces should: solve a real problem in the space, demonstrate your authority, and naturally lead to "the full system" you're about to launch. Every piece should link to your waitlist page. End every video with "I'm building something for people dealing with exactly this — link in bio/description."
2
Run a challenge, workshop, or free training
A free 3-5 day challenge or a single free live training is the fastest waitlist builder in the creator economy. Price: $0. Requirement: email signup. Topic: directly related to the product you're launching. End the free training with a preview of your paid offer. This warms your audience and proves your teaching ability at the same time.
3
Set a waitlist goal and track weekly
Minimum viable launch requires: 500 waitlist subscribers for a sub-$100 product, 200 waitlist subscribers for a $100-$500 product, 100 qualified waitlist subscribers for a $500+ product. If you're below these thresholds heading into Week 4, extend your pre-pre-launch by 2 additional weeks before opening the cart.

Social Proof Gathering (Pre-Launch)

3 Ways to Get Testimonials Before Launch
1. Give 5-10 people early access in exchange for honest written feedback. Ask specifically: "What result did you get? How long did it take? Who would you recommend this to?" Testimonials with a result + timeframe convert far better than general praise.

2. Mine your existing content comments for social proof. If someone commented "this changed how I think about [topic]" on a relevant video — screenshot it with permission and use it.

3. Survey your waitlist after they sign up: "What made you sign up?" The responses are often more persuasive than scripted testimonials because they're in your audience's own words and reflect their actual motivation.

Social Proof Capture System

Getting Testimonials That Actually Convert
Use this exact question in your Day-7 email or in a post-purchase survey (Typeform or Google Forms, linked in Onboarding Email 3):

"What was your situation before you got [PRODUCT NAME], and what has changed (or what do you now understand) after going through it?"

This question structure forces before/after framing, which is the structure that converts best in testimonials. Generic "I loved it, 5 stars" testimonials have almost no persuasive effect. A specific before/after story with a named outcome converts.

Refund Handling

Refund Protocol
Process all refund requests within 24 hours. No questions asked if within your stated policy window. When someone requests a refund, reply with: "No problem at all — refund processed. I'd love to understand why it wasn't the right fit if you're open to sharing, but there's no pressure and no requirement. I hope we cross paths again." This response generates goodwill, reduces chargeback risk, and occasionally keeps the customer (who reconsidered after feeling heard). Never fight refunds or require proof of "trying" the product — it damages your reputation far more than the lost revenue.

Upsell Opportunity: Week 2

When and How to Upsell
If you have a higher-ticket offer (coaching, a more advanced product, a community), introduce it in Onboarding Email 3 or as a standalone email in Week 2. The pitch: "If you've gone through [PRODUCT NAME] and you want support implementing it with direct feedback from me, [HIGHER OFFER] might be the right next step." Only pitch people who've had time to start and are engaged. Upselling to buyers before they've had a chance to open the product is a fast way to lose trust.

Conversion Rate Benchmarks by List Size

Waitlist-to-Buyer Conversion Benchmarks: Cold list (low engagement, minimal warm-up): 0.5–1.5% Average list (some warm-up, decent engagement): 2–4% Warm list (highly engaged, great pre-launch): 5–10% Hot list (community built, strong relationship): 10–20%+ Revenue Per Subscriber Benchmarks: Budget product ($27-$97): $1.50–$5.00 per waitlist sub Mid-range ($97-$297): $3.00–$10.00 per waitlist sub Premium ($297+): $8.00–$25.00+ per waitlist sub Email Sequence Benchmarks (launch week): Open rate: 35-55% (your list knows you and expects these) Click rate: 5-15% (highly engaged during launch window) Unsubscribe rate: 0.5-2% per email (3%+ = your content/offer misalignment) Sales Page Conversion: Warm traffic (from email): 3-8% is solid, 10%+ is excellent Cold traffic (from social): 0.5-2% typical, 3%+ is strong

What a "Successful" Launch Looks Like

Success Benchmarks by Scale
First launch (list under 500): A successful first launch generates 20-50 buyers. This is not a failure scenario — it's proof of concept. Your job is to learn, refine, and repeat with a larger list. Many 7-figure creator businesses started with a $2,100 launch.

Growing creator (list 500-2,000): Expect 25-120 buyers at 2-6% conversion. Revenue $2,500-$12,000 for a $97 product. This is the stage where testimonials start compounding and refund rates stabilize.

Established creator (list 2,000-10,000): Expect 80-500 buyers at 2-5% conversion. Revenue $8,000-$50,000 for a $100-$200 product. Launch optimization has significant ROI at this scale — improving conversion from 2% to 3% is a 50% revenue increase.

Large creator (list 10,000+): Six-figure launches are achievable with a well-optimized funnel, strong social proof, and list engagement above 30% open rate. At this scale, the email sequence quality and sales page conversion rate are the primary levers.

Post-Launch Debrief (Do This Within 1 Week of Close)

1
Pull all metrics into a single document
Waitlist size at launch, total buyers, conversion rate, total revenue, refund count, refund rate, email open rates by day, email click rates by day, best-performing email (highest click rate), worst-performing email.
2
Identify your conversion bottleneck
Low open rates = subject lines or sender name recognition issue. High open rates, low click rates = email copy issue (body didn't sell the click). High click rates, low sales page conversion = sales page issue. High conversion but low buyer numbers = list size/warmth issue. Fix the bottleneck first for next launch.
3
Collect all feedback and testimonials
Read every reply to every launch email. Note objections that came up more than twice — these go into your next FAQ email. Note praise that sounds specific — these become testimonials. Note confusion — these indicate clarity problems in the sales page or product.
4
Set the date for your next launch
The best time to plan your next launch is immediately after the current one closes. Set a date 60-90 days out. Start building toward it now.
Section 08

Re-Launch Strategy

Your first launch is proof of concept. Your second launch is optimization. Your third launch is a system. Here is how to think about each model.

Model 1: Live Launch — Open / Close Cart

Open/Close Cart Model
Launch 2-4 times per year, each with a defined open and close date. Benefits: urgency drives conversions (people buy when there's a deadline, not when it's always available), you can improve the product and email sequence between launches, and you build anticipation with each cycle.

Typical cadence: Open 2x/year for sub-$200 products. Open 1-2x/year for $200+ products. Each subsequent launch to the same audience converts at 30-50% lower than the first launch — compensate by growing your list between launches.

Between launches: run a waitlist, run a free challenge, publish consistently, and funnel every new subscriber into a waitlist sequence so they're primed before the cart opens.

Model 2: Evergreen Funnel (Always Open)

Evergreen Funnel Setup
An evergreen funnel lets new subscribers automatically go through your launch sequence on a rolling basis — as if every day is launch day for someone new. Setup:

1. Drive traffic to a lead magnet (free resource) that is directly related to your product.
2. Lead magnet subscribers go into an automated email sequence (your pre-launch warm-up emails + launch week emails, rewritten to work without real-time urgency).
3. Use a "pseudo-deadline" via Deadline Funnel or ThriveCart — each subscriber gets a personalized 7-day window from when they sign up, not a real launch date.
4. Product is always available at full price (no early-bird — those are live launch tactics only).

Evergreen funnels convert at lower rates (0.5-2% vs. 2-8% for live launches) but generate revenue 24/7 without ongoing effort. Best for proven products with strong social proof and a steady flow of new subscribers.

Model 3: Hybrid — Live Launch + Evergreen

The Recommended Model for Most Creators
Run 1-2 live launches per year for maximum revenue spikes and fresh social proof. Between launches, keep the product available via an evergreen funnel at full price (no discount — discounts are reserved for live launch urgency). This hybrid generates consistent baseline revenue between launches while preserving the emotional peak and conversion boost of live launch events.

Messaging for between launches: "This product is available now at the full price of $[PRICE]. When I run the next live launch in [TIMEFRAME], I'll open early-bird pricing for the waitlist first. If you want to join now at full price, [LINK]. If you want to wait for the next launch, [WAITLIST LINK]."

Re-Launch Improvements Checklist

What to Improve Before Each Re-Launch
— Update testimonials: Replace generic praise with specific before/after results gathered from your first buyer cohort
— Rewrite the subject line of your lowest-performing email from launch 1
— Update your sales page FAQ with objections that came up repeatedly in email replies
— Add any product improvements or new components since the last launch
— Review your waitlist building strategy — did it bring in the right people, or did you attract people who were never going to buy?
— Adjust your price if your conversion data supports it: under 1% conversion often signals the price is too high or the audience-product fit is off; over 10% conversion often signals you could charge more
— Create one new piece of launch content that doesn't exist yet: a case study, a comparison post, a video walkthrough of a product section
✓
The compounding launch: Each launch builds on the one before it. Better testimonials. A bigger list. A more refined email sequence. Reduced refund rate. By your third launch of the same product to a growing audience, the system largely runs itself — your job is to grow the list and show up in the emails. That is the long game.

Part 1

The Viral Content Framework

Virality isn't random. Every piece of content that spreads does so because it triggers one or more of six psychological drivers. Understanding these is the difference between hoping your content goes viral and engineering it.

The 6 Virality Drivers

Driver 1 — Identity Signal

People share content that says something about who they are. "This is so me." "This is exactly how I think." Content that lets someone signal their identity, values, or tribal membership to their audience gets shared because sharing it is itself the message. Ask: does sharing this content say something about the person sharing it?

Driver 2 — Emotional Activation

High-arousal emotions drive sharing. Awe, anger, anxiety, and excitement cause people to act. Low-arousal emotions (sadness, contentment) slow sharing. Design content to spike one of the four high-arousal emotions. Note: you don't need to be controversial — awe and excitement work just as well as outrage, with less blowback.

Driver 3 — Practical Value

"News you can use." Content that makes someone better at something, saves them money, or helps them solve a specific problem gets shared because sharing it is a gift. The more specific and immediately actionable the value, the more shareable it is. "5 ways to be more productive" is less shareable than "The 2-minute trick that eliminated 40% of my email time."

Driver 4 — Social Currency

People share things that make them look good — smart, informed, funny, or in-the-know. If sharing your content makes someone look like they're ahead of the curve, they'll share it. Contrarian takes, under-the-radar information, and insider knowledge all create social currency. Tip: position content as "most people don't know this."

Driver 5 — Story & Narrative

Humans are wired for story. Information delivered as story is 22× more memorable than information delivered as data. A good story has: a character the audience relates to, a conflict or obstacle, and a resolution with a takeaway. Short-form story works in 60 seconds. You don't need long content — you need narrative structure.

Driver 6 — Novelty & Surprise

The brain's reward system activates on novelty. Unexpected angles, surprising data points, and counterintuitive conclusions generate dopamine and keep attention. If your content confirms what people already believe, it's forgettable. If it flips an assumption, it's shareable. Pattern-interrupt early — in the first 3 seconds — or you've already lost them.

The ACER Content Structure

Use this 4-part structure for any piece of content — short or long. It's built around how attention actually works online.

A — Attention (0–3 seconds): Stop the scroll. The hook. Makes them stay.
C — Curiosity (3–15 seconds): Open a loop. Tease the payoff. Don't deliver yet.
E — Engagement (15 seconds – end): Deliver real value. Keep the loop open until the last moment.
R — Retention (end): Satisfy the loop. Give the CTA. Make them want to come back.
The most common mistake: Creators front-load context before the hook. "Hey guys, today we're going to talk about..." kills content before it starts. Start with the hook — every time, on every platform.

Part 3

Platform Format Guides

Each platform has its own physics. What works on LinkedIn breaks on TikTok. What dominates Twitter dies on YouTube. These guides map the formats, lengths, and behaviors that consistently perform on each platform.

TikTok

  • Hook in first 1–2 words (not first sentence — first words)
  • Best lengths: 21–34 seconds and 60–90 seconds (sweet spots for completion)
  • Text overlay on-screen for the first 3 seconds — catches silent viewers
  • End on a cliffhanger or loop point to increase rewatch rate
  • Trending sounds boost reach even for non-music content
  • Post between 7–9am or 7–9pm in your audience's timezone
  • Reply to comments with videos — these often outperform originals

Instagram Reels

  • Best lengths: 7–15 seconds (highest share rate) or 30–60 seconds
  • Captions matter more than on TikTok — add 3–5 sentences of context
  • First frame must work as a static image (previewed before autoplay)
  • Trending audio gives 2–3× boost — use it even quietly in background
  • Shares (DMs) are weighted more than likes — create "send this to someone who..." moments
  • Saves signal value — end with a reason to save ("screenshot this")

LinkedIn

  • First line determines whether anyone clicks "see more" — make it a standalone hook
  • Single line breaks between every 1–2 sentences (no dense paragraphs)
  • Best formats: personal story, numbered lessons, contrarian take
  • First 60 minutes of engagement heavily determines reach
  • Comments beat likes for distribution — end with a discussion question
  • Don't include links in the post body — add in first comment instead
  • Best times: Tuesday–Thursday, 8–10am

Twitter / X

  • Thread Tweet 1 is everything — it must work as a complete, shareable thought
  • Threads: each tweet should end with a reason to read the next one
  • Images add reach — even simple text-on-image outperforms text-only
  • Replies to large accounts in your niche drive more new followers than original posts
  • Ratio matters — aim for 2× replies vs likes (shows conversation)
  • Best engagement: 8–10am and 6–9pm weekdays

Part 4

The Repurposing Matrix

One piece of long-form content should produce 8–12 platform-native pieces. This isn't cross-posting — it's extracting different value angles from the same source material and reformatting each for the platform it lives on.

The rule: Never just copy-paste content between platforms. Each platform has native behavior. A YouTube video description posted as a LinkedIn update will flop. The same insight, reformatted as a LinkedIn story, can go viral. Extract the idea — reformat completely.

The Core Repurposing Flow

Long-Form Anchor (YouTube/Podcast/Blog) → Short Clips + Quotes + Key Insights → Platform-Specific Formats → Distribution → Email Digest

Weekly Content Engine (One Anchor → Full Week)

Create one anchor piece per week. Run it through this workflow and you'll have daily content across all platforms without creating anything new.

  • Monday: Publish anchor content (YouTube / blog / podcast). Send email to list.
  • Tuesday: Post best short clip on TikTok and Reels. LinkedIn story post on the main lesson.
  • Wednesday: Twitter thread on the key framework or insight. Instagram carousel outline.
  • Thursday: Instagram carousel posted. Behind-the-scenes or process clip on Reels/Stories.
  • Friday: Second short clip (different angle). Twitter single-tweet insight from the same content.
  • Saturday: Quote image or micro-lesson on Instagram/LinkedIn. TikTok response to comments from Monday.
  • Sunday: Prep next week's anchor. Review what performed best — double down on that angle.

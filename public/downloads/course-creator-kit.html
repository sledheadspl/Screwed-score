Phase 0

Validation: Prove Demand Before You Build a Single Lesson

The graveyard of online courses is full of content nobody asked for. Before you record a single video, spend 1–2 weeks validating that real people will pay real money for your specific transformation.

The Presell Method — Sell Before You Build

The presell method is the single most important concept in course creation. Instead of building your course, then hoping people buy it, you sell access to the course before it exists — then build it based on confirmed demand and real student questions.

How It Works

  1. Write a 1-paragraph course concept — what transformation does it deliver, who is it for, what's the price?
  2. Create a simple sales page — a Google Doc, Notion page, or basic landing page. No design required.
  3. Set a presell price 20–30% below launch price — reward early believers. Frame it as "Founding Member Pricing."
  4. Set a minimum threshold — "If 20 people join by [date], I'll build it. If not, you get a full refund." This removes your risk and theirs.
  5. Drive traffic via direct outreach — email your list, post on social, DM warm leads. No ads needed at this stage.
  6. Collect payment via Stripe or Gumroad — hold funds in a separate account until threshold is met.
  7. If threshold is hit, build — deliver content weekly or in cohort batches, incorporating live Q&A to fill gaps.

Real talk: If you cannot sell 10–20 presell spots at $97–$297 to people who already know you, a $997 evergreen course to cold traffic will not save you. Fix the offer first.

10 Validation Signals to Look For

Before you presell, look for these signals that a course idea has legs. The more you can check off, the stronger the opportunity.

  1. People are already paying for adjacent solutions — books, coaching, software, conferences about this topic exist and are selling.
  2. You get the same question repeatedly — if 5+ people have asked you how to do the same thing, that's a course waiting to be built.
  3. The outcome is specific and measurable — "get a remote job as a UX designer in 90 days" beats "learn design."
  4. The audience has a budget — B2B skills, career advancement, revenue-generating skills all attract buyers with money. Hobbies are harder.
  5. There are active communities around the topic — Reddit threads, Facebook groups, Discord servers all full of questions = demand.
  6. Competitors exist and are succeeding — competition validates the market. No competition often means no market.
  7. The pain is urgent — people in active pain buy faster. "I need a job now" converts better than "I'd like to improve someday."
  8. You have a credibility hook — you've done the thing, helped others do the thing, or have a unique methodological angle.
  9. The topic has recurring search volume — check Ahrefs, Google Trends, or AnswerThePublic. Flat or growing is good. Declining is a red flag.
  10. Your audience already trusts you — an email list, social following, or community of 500+ engaged people is a major advantage.

Pricing Research Process

Set your price based on data, not what feels comfortable. Here's the process:

Step 1: Competitive Landscape Audit

Find 8–12 courses on your topic. Record: price, what's included, who the creator is, how long the course is, and what reviews say. Build a simple spreadsheet. You're looking for the pricing band most courses cluster in — and any gap you can own.

Step 2: Value-Based Pricing Calculation

Ask: what is the outcome of this course worth to the student? If your course helps someone land a $20,000/year raise, pricing at $997 is a no-brainer. If it saves someone 40 hours of frustration, price accordingly. Rule of thumb: your course price should be 1–10% of the value of the outcome.

Step 3: Customer Interviews

Talk to 10 people in your target audience. Ask them: "If a course guaranteed [specific outcome], what would you expect to pay for it?" Note the range. Also ask: "What's the most you've spent on professional development in the last 12 months?" This calibrates their spending willingness.

Step 4: Anchor Up, Discount Down

Always establish a higher anchor price, then discount for the launch or presell. A course listed at $997 with a launch price of $597 is more compelling than a course listed at $597 flat — even though the buyer pays less either way.

Phase 1

Curriculum Architecture

Most course creators either cram too much in or leave students confused about what they actually get. This phase gives you the structural blueprint for a course that delivers results and earns great reviews.

The 3-Act Course Structure

Borrow from storytelling: every great course has a beginning (orientation + quick win), a middle (the real work), and an end (transformation + next steps).

Act 1 — Foundation (20% of content)

Orient students. Set expectations. Deliver a quick win in the first lesson so students feel momentum immediately. Cover mindset, terminology, and the framework overview. If students don't feel progress in Module 1, they disengage forever.

Act 2 — Core System (60% of content)

This is where the transformation happens. Each module builds on the last. Use a logical progression: concepts → frameworks → demonstrations → exercises. Don't skip steps. Don't assume knowledge. Don't introduce a concept without immediately showing how to apply it.

Act 3 — Integration + Next Steps (20% of content)

Bring everything together. Show how the pieces connect. Give students a capstone project or final exercise. Celebrate what they've achieved. Point to what comes next (upsell, community, coaching). Students who finish feel accomplished and leave reviews.

The Transformation Framework

Your course is not a collection of information. It is a vehicle for transformation. Define both endpoints with precision before you plan a single lesson.

Before State — Where Students Start

Write out, in the student's own words, exactly where they are before they take your course. What are they struggling with? What have they tried that hasn't worked? What does their typical day look like? What's the pain? What's the cost of staying stuck?

After State — Where Students End Up

Write out the exact transformation. Be specific. Not "they understand marketing" but "they have a repeatable client acquisition system generating 3–5 qualified leads per week without paid ads." The after state drives every curriculum decision.

The Bridge — Your Course

Every module, lesson, and exercise should be a step across the bridge from before to after. If a piece of content doesn't move students from before to after, cut it. This is the hardest discipline in curriculum design.

Transformation Framework Worksheet

Before State:My student currently struggles with ___ and feels ___ every time they try to ___
Root Cause:The reason they're stuck is ___ (not ___ which is what they think it is)
After State:After the course, they can ___ consistently, and they feel ___ when doing it
Proof of Success:They'll know the course worked when ___ happens in their life/business
Timeline:A motivated student will achieve this in ___ days/weeks

How to Scope Your Course (Not Too Big, Not Too Small)

Scope creep is the #1 reason courses never launch. Here's how to find the right scope:

The "One Promise" Rule

Your course should deliver one specific, measurable promise. If you can't state the outcome in a single sentence, the scope is too big. Cut modules until you can.

Signs Your Course Is Too Big

  • You have more than 8 modules
  • Total video content exceeds 10 hours
  • Students need to learn multiple fundamentally different skill sets
  • You keep saying "and then there's also..."
  • You've been building it for more than 3 months

Signs Your Course Is Too Small

  • You can cover the entire topic in under 90 minutes
  • There's nothing for students to practice or implement
  • The outcome could be achieved with a single blog post
  • You're struggling to justify a price above $97

The Goldilocks Zone

For a $497–$997 course: 5–8 modules, 3–5 lessons each, 5–10 minutes per lesson, total completion time 4–8 hours. Students can complete it in a focused weekend or spread over 4 weeks at one module per week.

Learning Objective Writing Formula

Vague objectives produce vague content. Use this formula for every module and lesson:

Formula: "By the end of this [module/lesson], students will be able to [ACTION VERB] + [SPECIFIC OBJECT] + [MEASURABLE CONDITION OR STANDARD]"

Strong Action Verbs by Level

LevelGood VerbsAvoid
Knowledgeidentify, list, recall, name, defineknow, understand, be aware of
Comprehensionexplain, summarize, describe, interpretlearn, grasp, appreciate
Applicationuse, apply, build, execute, complete, implementunderstand how to, be familiar with
Analysisanalyze, diagnose, compare, distinguish, auditthink about, consider
Creationcreate, design, write, produce, develop, launchmake (too vague)

Examples

  • Weak: "Understand email marketing." Strong: "Write a 5-email welcome sequence that achieves 40%+ open rates."
  • Weak: "Learn about pricing." Strong: "Set a project rate using the value-based pricing formula and defend it confidently on client calls."
  • Weak: "Know how to use SEO." Strong: "Identify 10 low-competition, high-intent keywords using Ahrefs and write optimized title tags for each."
Phase 2

Lesson Structure Formula

Every lesson should follow the same underlying structure, even if it doesn't feel formulaic to the student. Consistency of structure creates momentum. Students always know what to expect and never feel lost.

The 5-Part Lesson Framework

  1. Hook (30–60 seconds) — Open with a question, a provocative statement, or a mini-story that makes the student immediately invested. "By the end of this lesson, you'll know the one pricing mistake that cost me my first $20K client — and exactly how to avoid it." Never open with "Hi, welcome back to module 3 lesson 2."
  2. Teach (40–60% of lesson time) — Deliver the core concept. Use the "explain → example → explain again" loop. State the principle, show it in action, then restate the principle in light of what they just saw. Keep language conversational. Avoid academic tone.
  3. Demonstrate (20–30% of lesson time) — Show it, don't just tell it. Screen share a real example. Walk through a before/after. Do the exercise live. The demonstration is where abstract concepts become concrete skills.
  4. Apply (5–10 minutes at end) — Give students a specific action to take before the next lesson. "Before you move on, complete [specific task]. This takes about 10 minutes and is the foundation for everything in the next module." Students who don't apply learn nothing.
  5. Review (60–90 seconds) — Summarize the 2–3 key takeaways. Preview what's coming next to create a cliffhanger. "In the next lesson, we're going to take what you just built and run it through our 5-point audit — you'll probably find 2 things you need to fix before sending this to a client."

Slide Design Principles for Course Creators

Your slides are teaching tools, not documents. They support your voice — they don't replace it.

  • One idea per slide. If a slide has more than one concept, split it. Cognitive overload kills retention.
  • Max 30 words per slide. If you need more words, use them in your voiceover — not on the slide.
  • Use the "billboard test." If someone driving 60mph couldn't read your slide in 3 seconds, it has too much on it.
  • Contrast is king. Dark background + light text or vice versa. Never gray-on-gray. Accessibility matters.
  • Use visuals to show process. Arrows, diagrams, before/after comparisons all teach better than bullet lists.
  • Brand consistency builds perceived value. Pick 2 fonts and 3 colors. Use them everywhere. Students pay attention to polish.
  • Talking head + slides = best retention. When possible, use picture-in-picture so students see your face and the slides simultaneously.

Tool stack: Canva (slides), Loom (screen + webcam recording), Descript (editing), or Keynote/PowerPoint if you prefer. Don't buy a $3,000 camera before you have 10 paying students.

Workbook & Exercise Creation Guide

Workbooks transform passive watchers into active learners — and active learners get results, leave reviews, and buy your next course.

What Belongs in a Workbook

  • Fill-in-the-blank sections that mirror lesson content (forces engagement)
  • Reflection prompts — "What's your biggest takeaway?" / "Where does this apply to your situation?"
  • Action checklists — specific tasks to complete before moving on
  • Templates and frameworks students fill out for their own situation
  • Space for notes, sketches, and examples

Workbook Design Tips

  • Make it printable AND digital-fillable (use PDF form fields)
  • Brand it with your course name — students screenshot these and share them
  • Include a progress tracker on the first page
  • Use page numbers that match lesson numbers so it's easy to find

Engagement Loop Techniques

Engagement in courses is not about entertainment — it's about creating the conditions for continued action. Use these techniques throughout your course:

  • Progress visibility — show students how far they've come and how far they have to go. Platforms like Kajabi and Teachable do this with progress bars. Students are far more likely to finish when they can see the finish line.
  • Micro-commitments — ask students to check off each lesson as complete. The act of checking a box releases dopamine and primes them for the next one.
  • Community integration — at the end of each module, prompt students to share their work in the community. "Post your completed exercise in #module-2-wins." Social accountability dramatically increases completion rates.
  • Email check-ins — automated emails at day 3, day 7, and day 14 that reference specific lessons and ask a question. "Hey, did you get through Module 2 yet? What was your biggest aha moment?" This triggers re-engagement for students who've drifted.
  • Quick wins early and often — schedule at least one moment of visible progress in every module. Students who feel momentum continue. Students who feel lost disappear.
Phase 3

Pricing Strategy

Price is a signal, not just a number. How you price your course communicates who it's for, what it's worth, and how serious you are. Price too low and you attract the wrong buyers. Price correctly and you attract people who actually do the work.

The Price Anchoring Technique

Anchoring is the single most powerful pricing lever available to you. Here's how to deploy it:

Method 1: The "Coaching Alternative" Anchor

Show what it would cost to get the same result through a more expensive alternative. "Private 1:1 coaching with me is $500/hour. Most clients need 8–12 sessions to achieve what this course delivers. That's $4,000–$6,000. Today, you're getting the complete system for $997." The $997 feels like a steal.

Method 2: The Strikethrough Price

Show the "regular" price crossed out, then show the launch price. The regular price must be real — something you'll actually charge after launch. Don't fabricate it. But the gap between $1,497 and $697 is extremely compelling during a launch window.

Method 3: Price Per Day

Break the price into daily cost over the expected transformation period. "$997 for a 90-day transformation is $11/day — less than your morning coffee. You're betting $11/day on yourself." This works especially well when combined with a payment plan.

3-Tier Pricing Structure

Launch Pricing vs. Evergreen Pricing

Launch Pricing

During an active launch (open cart for 5–10 days), offer a meaningful discount to reward early action. 30–40% off is a strong launch incentive. Add real deadlines — cart closes at midnight Friday, price goes to $X — and honor those deadlines without exception. False scarcity destroys trust.

Evergreen Pricing

Once the launch closes, raise to full price. Evergreen buyers pay full price and often convert via automated webinars, email sequences triggered by lead magnet opt-ins, or direct sales calls. Your evergreen funnel should target a cold audience landing on your course page via ads, SEO, or affiliate traffic.

Founding Member Pricing (Best for First Launch)

Your first cohort should receive "founding member" pricing — typically 40–50% off lifetime — in exchange for feedback, testimonials, and patience as you refine the curriculum. Frame it as a privilege, not a discount. These students become your most loyal advocates.

Payment Plan Psychology and Structure

Payment plans increase conversions by 20–40% on courses priced above $497. Here's how to structure them effectively:

The Math

Charge a premium for payment plans. If your course is $997, a 3-pay plan should be $397 × 3 = $1,191 — a 19% premium. This is standard, accepted, and fair. Students who want the lower effective price choose to pay in full. Students who need cash flow flexibility pay the premium. Everyone wins.

Get a clear terms of service agreement signed at checkout. Specify that students who access course content are bound to complete all payments. Use Stripe's subscription billing so payments auto-collect. Have a dunning process (automated payment failure emails) in place before launch.

Phase 8

Post-Launch: Evergreen Funnel, Affiliates & Course Updates

A successful launch is not the end of the story — it's proof of concept. Now you build the machine that sells the course while you sleep.

Evergreen Funnel Architecture

An evergreen funnel allows your course to sell continuously, without a live launch. Here's the standard high-converting architecture:

  1. Traffic Source — Organic social, SEO blog content, YouTube, podcast appearances, or paid ads. All traffic flows to a single lead magnet opt-in page.
  2. Lead Magnet — A free resource that solves a specific micro-problem for your target student. Examples: a checklist, a template, a short guide, a mini-course, a quiz. Must be directly related to the paid course topic. Deliver it instantly after opt-in.
  3. Email Welcome Sequence (5–7 emails over 7–10 days) — Build relationship, deliver value, introduce the paid course offer. This is a condensed version of your launch sequence — same structure, evergreen content. End with a hard CTA to the sales page.
  4. Evergreen Webinar or VSL — A recorded webinar or video sales letter (30–60 minutes) that delivers a mini training and pitches the course at the end. Services like EverWebinar or WebinarJam automate this. Alternatively, a YouTube video or Loom with a clear CTA works for simpler funnels.
  5. Sales Page with Evergreen Urgency — Use a tool like Deadline Funnel to give each subscriber a personalized 3–5 day cart window. When they opt in, the clock starts. Their sales page shows a real countdown timer that expires for them personally. This creates genuine urgency without a live launch.
  6. Post-Purchase Onboarding — Immediately after purchase, the onboarding sequence from Phase 6 kicks in. Great onboarding = completion = testimonials = word-of-mouth referrals = more organic traffic.
  7. Retargeting Ads — Serve retargeting ads on Facebook/Instagram/YouTube to people who visited your sales page but didn't buy. Budget: $15–$30/day. These are your warmest prospects and the cheapest conversions you'll get from paid media.

Funnel economics benchmark: A healthy evergreen funnel converts 1–3% of email opt-ins to paid course buyers. At $997/course and 1,000 opt-ins/month at 2% conversion = 20 sales = $19,940/month. At $15/lead (reasonable for a warm content-driven audience), that's $15,000 in ad spend for $19,940 in revenue. Profitable and scalable.

How to Recruit Affiliates

  1. Start with your students — email graduates who got results. A student saying "this worked for me" converts better than any other marketing. Offer a bonus commission rate for students.
  2. Find complementary-audience creators — who serves the same audience as you but doesn't compete directly? A freelance course + a tools review blog, for example. Pitch them with your conversion data and their potential earnings.
  3. List on affiliate marketplaces — Commission Junction, ShareASale, Impact, or the platform-native affiliate marketplace (Teachable, Kajabi). Passive discovery from motivated affiliates.
  4. Run a launch affiliate program — recruit affiliates specifically for your live launch, offer a bonus prize for the top affiliate (cash, product credit, 1:1 coaching). Leaderboards create competition and motivation.

Course Update Cadence

A living course is a better course — and updating it is one of the easiest ways to maintain premium pricing, earn re-enrollment, and keep your testimonials current.

Quarterly Light Review

Every quarter: review student Q&A logs and community questions for recurring confusion. If the same question comes up 5+ times, that lesson needs to be clearer or a new lesson needs to be added. Update templates and tool references as platforms change.

Annual Deep Review

Once per year: review the entire curriculum end-to-end. Remove outdated content. Add new modules based on what you've learned from students. Update case studies and examples. This is also the moment to raise your evergreen price — "Updated for [year], with X new modules" is a legitimate value increase.

How to Notify Students of Updates

  • Email all current students with a changelog — what was added, what was updated, what was removed
  • Post in the community with a summary and encourage students to revisit updated modules
  • For major updates, consider hosting a live "what's new" Q&A call for active students
  • Use the update as a re-launch marketing moment — "The course just got better, and here's how"

Version Control for Your Course

Keep a simple changelog document (Google Doc or Notion page) that records every change with the date. This is invaluable for communicating value to students, for your own reference, and for making the case for price increases. "Version 3.0 — 40% new content, updated for [year]" is a compelling upsell or re-enrollment pitch to past students who might want the new material.

The long game: Course creators who build once and never update see declining conversion rates and stale reviews. Course creators who treat their course as a living product compound their authority, their alumni network, and their revenue every year. Commit to the update cadence before you launch.
